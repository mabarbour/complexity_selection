Evolution by Natural Selection

Requires 3 ingredients:

  • Variation in phenotypes
  • Variation in fitness
  • Heritable phenotypic variation

Quantifying Natural Selection


Selection differential: change in mean phenotype, \( \bar{z} \), after selection

\[ S=\bar{z}^*-\bar{z} \]


Standardized Selection Differential

\[ S'=\frac{S}{\sigma_z}=B'=0.39 \]

\[ B=\frac{S}{\sigma^2_z}=0.18 \]

\( \sigma_z \): standard deviation of the phenotype before selection

\( \sigma^2_z \): variance of the phenotype before selection

Alternative Calculation of Selection Differential


Covariance between relative fitness \( w \) and the phenotype:

\[ S=cov(w,z) \]

Relative fitness is calculated as absolute fitness (\( W \)) divided by the mean fitness of the population: \[ w=\frac{W}{\bar{W}} \]

This becomes especially useful when we want to quantify selection acting on multiple traits. This approach enables use to calculate selection gradients, which corresponds to the direct effects of selection. Selection differentials, on the other hand, quantify both the direct and indirect effects of selection when multiple traits are considered (because they cannot take into account selection imposed by other traits).

Note that for a single phenotype, the selection differential corresponds to the selection gradient \( \beta \).

Alternative Calculation of Selection Differential

Intercept B B' = B * sd(z) S = B * var(z)
Estimate 1 0.18 0.39 0.83

Note that \( S'=\beta' \).
